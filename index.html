Hi, I'm Alexander Nolte

I am an Associate Professor at the University of Tartu and an Adjunct Associate Professor at Carnegie Mellon University. My research takes place at the intersection between human computer interaction, information systems and software engineering and focuses on understanding and developing means to support collaboration in corporate, entrepreneurial, civic, educational, and scientific settings.

Research

I believe in rigorous, interdisciplinary research on topics that are relevant to individuals, teams, organizations, communities and society as a whole.

My work is grounded in socio-technical systems theory and takes place at the intersection between human computer interaction, software engineering and information systems. I primarily focus on two intertwined research lines: (1) hybrid collaboration settings and (2) human-AI interaction. To that end I utilize a mixed-methods approach that combines qualitative methodology like interviews, questionnaires, and observations with quantitative analytics.

Hybrid collaboration settings

I mainly focus on integrating different forms of collaboration such as hackathons into corporate, entrepreneurial, civic, educational, and scientific settings. My aim is to understand how the design of such events can foster the sustainability of outcomes such as innovative products and services, individuals and collaborative learning, community engagement, and others. I have developed a multi-level methodological open-source framework to help hackathon planners navigate design decisions (hackathon-planning-kit.org).

Human-AI interaction

I currently focus on two domains. On the one hand, I study the interaction of individuals with autonomous ride-hailing vehicles. I develop and evaluate mechanisms that supports human vehicle interaction on the outside and inside. On the other hand, I study how analysts utilize AI to analyze work and business processes. I mainly focus on the understandability of AI suggestions and on aspects that can foster analysts’ including suggestions into their decision-making process. In this context I have co-developed RuM (rulemining.org/) – an open-source toolkit for declarative process mining which provides visual and speech-based interfaces for analysts to utilize AI driven process analytics.

Teaching

In my courses, my aim is to support students in acquiring much-needed skills for the 21st century learner, such as critical thinking, problem solving and data literacy skills. At the same time, I encourage students to learn from and with each other in a social arena. I combine formal lectures for transferring information and hands-on workshops to foster communication and knowledge building on common ground.
